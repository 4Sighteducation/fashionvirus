## 1. The pitch

**Build a fashion empire. Face the fallout.**

## 2. The intellectual frame

Karen's references aren't background reading. Each one solves a live design problem, and together they're what makes this a serious work rather than an eco-game.

### Norbert Wiener — *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950)
The founding text of cybernetics: systems, feedback loops, and what happens to people inside machines that optimise.

**What it gives us:** the justification for the entire architecture. This is a game about a *system*, not about villains. Nobody in the fashion supply chain is evil; everyone is responding rationally to feedback. The player never chooses to destroy a river — they choose a colour that sells. Wiener is why the game has no antagonist and needs none.

**Mechanically:** feedback loops must be *visible as loops*. Growth demands novelty; novelty demands volume; volume demands cost reduction; cost reduction demands opacity; opacity enables the damage. The player should feel the loop tighten around them.

### E. F. Schumacher — *Small Is Beautiful* (1973)
Appropriate and intermediate technology. Human-scale economics. Production that fits the place it happens in.

**What it gives us:** the vocabulary for **Act 2's repair options**, which were previously vague. Repair isn't "spend money on green." It's Schumacher: local, small-scale, appropriate to context, owned by the people doing it. Repair hubs. Regional mills. Tools people can maintain themselves.

**Mechanically:** the repair menu is explicitly *intermediate technology* — cheap, local, human-scale interventions that are slow and unglamorous and actually work. And this is also the answer to §8's "perfect run": Schumacher is why the good ending is **small**, not big.

### Marge Piercy — *Woman on the Edge of Time* (1976)
Utopia and dystopia running in parallel, reachable from the same present. Prescient about networked communication. And — crucially for us — it describes what **clothing looks like in a good society**: made to last, repaired, shared, ceremonial, beautiful.

**What it gives us:** the thing v0.2 was missing — **a positive vision with actual content.** We knew what the bad ending looked like. We had nothing concrete for the good one. Piercy supplies it: not "less fashion," but *different* fashion — durable, communal, repaired, meaningful, still beautiful.

**Mechanically:** the sustainable path must produce *visible aesthetic culture*, not austerity. The reward for playing well is not a smaller number — it's a world with **better clothes in it**. That reframes the whole clean path from sacrifice to alternative.

### Edward Burtynsky — *Manufactured Landscapes*
Large-format photography of industrial landscapes: tailings ponds, shipbreaking yards, quarries — and, directly relevant, vast garment factory floors.

**What it gives us:** **the art direction for Act 2, solved.** Burtynsky's method is the exact tonal register we need — the images are *beautiful*, sublime even, and that beauty is what makes them unbearable. He never editorialises. He shows scale, and scale does the work.

**Mechanically/visually:** Act 2 is wide, still, aerial, quiet. Vast compositions where the human figure is tiny. Colour that is genuinely gorgeous and completely wrong. **No music swelling, no text telling you it's sad.** Scale and silence. This supersedes the "risograph" suggestion in v0.2 — Burtynsky is a much better answer.

> *(Influence and register only — we don't reproduce his images. But the approach is exactly right, and naming it in a funding bid signals seriousness.)*

### And the ancestor: World Factory
METIS / Zoë Svendsen & Simon Daw, 2015. Card-based scenario game-theatre; audiences run a Chinese clothing factory; QR codes at the end reveal the true environmental cost. **We should approach Svendsen early and warmly** — see §13.

## 3. Two player types, one world

Karen's framing, and it's right: the game serves two audiences with the same systems.

**The growth player** — speed, profit, scale. Frostpunk and Plague Inc. players. They want to optimise and they want stakes.

**The sustainability player** — circularity, repair, ethics. Students, practitioners, people who want to *test ideas in a living system*.

Both play the same game. Both shape the same world. **The world remembers.**

### How Act 2 differs by behaviour

| | Growth play | Sustainable play |
|---|---|---|
| Rivers | Toxic, dead | Some remain clean |
| Biodiversity | Gone | Pockets survive |
| Communities | Sick, displaced | Resilient |
| Horizon | Landfill | Working landscape |
| Repair cost | Enormous | Affordable |
| Recoverable | Almost nothing | Some zones |
| Tone | Bleak, desperate | Hard, not hopeless |

**Neither is easy. Neither world is magically fine.** The sustainable player still inherits a damaged world — because they operated inside a damaged system. That honesty is the point.

## 4. Structure — three movements

### ACT 1 — "FASHION VIRUS"
Bedroom label → indie → high street → global. Glossy, seductive, fast.

The player chooses: materials, labour conditions, production speed, waste streams, energy, repair vs disposal, rental vs retail, transparency vs exploitation.

A **hidden ledger** — water, emissions, microplastics, waste, land, labour — accumulates where the player cannot see it, glimpsed only in peripheral whispers they're incentivised to ignore. **The interface itself degrades** as damage mounts, slowly enough that nobody consciously notices.

### THE HINGE — "THE REVEAL"
Enormous, sincere, lavish fanfare. Awards, valuation, covers, a standing ovation. **Take the celebration seriously** — it must feel genuinely good.

Then the interface falls away, and the world you built becomes the world you must walk into.

### ACT 2 — "WASTEWORLD"
Traverse it. Survive it. Repair what you can. Face what you can't.

> **Destruction is fast. Repair is slow. And partial.**
> That asymmetry is what makes the game honest.

**The two paths at the hinge:**

**CARRY ON** — keep chasing money. The mechanics still work; you can still make money. But this path **always ends in ruin** — not by authorial punishment, but by systems: environmental disaster, political upheaval, investor scandal, boycott. Each crisis can be deferred (PR, lobbying, relocation, a fresh pledge) and each deferral **costs more and buys less time**. A debt spiral. Ends in financial ruin or environmental catastrophe — and both should be spectacular and, frankly, fun.

**CHANGE** — reverse the damage. Slower, harder, poorer. Genuinely possible. See §6.

## 5. ⚠️ The one amendment — protect the reveal

**Do not let the player choose a "player type" at the start.**

If the game announces up front that there's a sustainable way to play, we lose the entire complicity engine. The power of Act 1 is that the player *doesn't know they're doing anything wrong* — they're just running a business well. Signposting the virtuous path turns the game into a quiz with a right answer, and the hinge becomes a punchline the player saw coming.

**How we serve both audiences without destroying the trick:**

**First playthrough: no mode select.** Everyone plays the same game blind. Some players will, of their own instinct, make careful choices — and the systems reward that honestly. Others will optimise for growth. Neither was told which was which. That's the whole design.

**The sustainability sandbox is the *second* playthrough.** Once you've seen the reveal, you know. Replay becomes the deliberate laboratory: *now* try to build a circular model, knowing what's coming. This is where Karen's "platform to test ideas" audience genuinely lives — and it's **better** post-reveal, because now every choice is informed.

**Add a New Game+ with the ledger visible.** After the first reveal, unlock a mode where the hidden ledger is on screen from turn one. That's the teaching tool, the exhibition mode, the classroom version. It's also a real reward for finishing.

> This costs us nothing and protects the single best moment in the game.
> **The reveal only works once. Spend it carefully.**

## 6. The repair vocabulary (Schumacher, made mechanical)

Act 2's repair options were vague in v0.2. Schumacher fixes that. Every repair should be **local, small-scale, appropriate, and owned by the people using it**:

- **Repair hubs** — neighbourhood-scale, low-tech, employ local people
- **Regional micro-mills** — production that fits its place
- **Take-back and remanufacture** — at a scale a town can run
- **Bioremediation** — slow, cheap, biological river recovery
- **Land rest and rewilding** — costs nothing but time, which you don't have
- **Skills transfer** — teaching repair, which compounds
- **Commons ownership** — hand the infrastructure over; it outlasts you

**None of these are glamorous. All of them are slow. That's the argument.**

### Making the slow burn legible
- **Flip the ledger.** The glossy dashboard is replaced by a restoration ledger — same number-go-up addiction, opposite direction.
- **Leading indicators.** The river won't run clean for twenty turns, but dissolved oxygen ticks up. Then one turn, quiet, full screen: ***"A kingfisher was seen on the river today."*** The most emotional moment in the game.
- **Some things never come back.** A species. A worker. A river that needs two hundred years. **The unfixable is what makes the fixable mean anything.**
- **Named people return.** The worker you underpaid in Act 1 reappears in Act 2.
- **Piercy's reward:** the clean path produces *visible culture* — clothes made to last, repaired, shared, ceremonial. Beautiful. The reward is not austerity.

## 7. The engine: surface and depth

**Every decision has a face value and a truth. Looking costs a turn.**

> *A hedge fund offers £500,000.*
> **[ ACCEPT ]** — cash now.
> **[ LEARN MORE ]** — costs one turn.
> *…they also fund controversial animal research.*

Eight turns later, a journalist makes the link. Reputational collapse.

**Due diligence is expensive — which is exactly why nobody does it.** The player can't say *"I didn't know."* Only *"I didn't look."*

This is Wiener's point rendered as a button: the system doesn't hide information, it just makes attention costly.

---

## 8. Architecture — cards and fuses

Cards carry a **surface** offer and an optional **depth**. Choices **plant tagged fuses**; consequences fire on *tags*, not on specific cards — so a journalist story can be triggered by any of eight different decisions.

Authored content, procedural consequence. ~70 cards, ~20 drawn per run, weighted by scale and prior choices. Reigns and Slay the Spire work this way. **Karen's research is the card corpus, and that's the defensible asset.**

## 9. Aesthetic

**Act 1 — the magazine that rots.** Glossy high-fashion editorial meets startup dashboard. Bold type, white space, slick animation. Seductive on purpose. As damage accumulates the aesthetic corrupts: whites go grey, a sickly yellow-green creeps in, grain and chemical bloom gather at the edges, the elegant typography smudges like a cheap print run. **The UI is complicit and the player is staring at it the whole time.**

**Act 2 — Burtynsky.** Wide, still, aerial, quiet. Vast compositions, tiny human figures. Gorgeous colour that is completely wrong. Scale does the emotional work; no swelling music, no text telling you how to feel.

## 10. Can you play the perfect game from the start?

**Yes. Brutally hard. Almost nobody manages it first time — and that's the replay hook.**

The pressure is the **novelty trap**: a demand meter that decays every turn and must be fed. Dirty way — more product, faster. Clean way — rental, resale, repair, upcycling: feeds it slowly, at thin margins, and you stay small while competitors eat your lunch.

**The perfect run does not end in an empire.** It ends:

> ### *"You made £3 million. The river still runs."*

**Preserve the World Factory nuance:** sometimes the decent thing pays *better* — it's just harder to find. Ethics should be **complicated**, not merely expensive. That's what stops it becoming a lecture.

## 11. Proof-of-concept scope

Browser-based, playable from a link, 15–20 minutes. **Model first, beauty second.**

- **Act 1, deep:** ~12 turns, 5 decision categories, 25–30 cards, the full hidden ledger, whispers, the greenwashing lever, named recurring characters, crude aesthetic degradation
- **The hinge, polished:** fanfare then reveal. This is the money shot.
- **Act 2, a wound not a game:** three turns. Arrive poor. Choose a repair. It costs enormously and fixes almost nothing. Then: *"The full game continues here."*
- **Telemetry:** anonymous decision logs. *How many players ever clicked LEARN MORE?* That's research data and funding evidence.

**Tech:** React / TypeScript / Supabase / Vercel. No engine, no 3D. The engine question is revisited after the design is proven.
